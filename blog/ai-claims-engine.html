From 16 Hours to 10 Seconds: How We Built an AI Claims Engine

April 28, 2025 7 min read AI, InsurTech, Product

When I joined Visit Health, the cashless insurance claim approval process took an average of 16 hours. For a policyholder standing at a hospital reception desk needing surgery, 16 hours isn't a minor inconvenience. It's a crisis.

Eighteen months later, that number was under 10 seconds — with 70% of claims processed entirely without human intervention. Here's how we got there, and what I learned about building AI products in a domain where the stakes are genuinely high.

The Real Problem (It Wasn't Speed)

The temptation when you hear "16 hours" is to think the problem is latency — that we needed faster servers or more agents. That framing was wrong, and chasing it would have gotten us nowhere.

The actual problem was decision-making under incomplete information. Insurance adjusters were making judgment calls on claims that had ambiguous documentation, edge-case policy language, or missing supporting records. Most of the delay wasn't processing — it was waiting: waiting for a document, waiting for a supervisor sign-off, waiting because the policy said "at the discretion of the adjudicator."

💡 Key Insight

When we mapped the 16-hour journey, we found that actual adjudicator "thinking time" averaged 12 minutes per claim. The rest was queue time, waiting for documents, and escalation overhead.

This reframing changed everything. The question became: which claims are actually ambiguous, and which ones just feel ambiguous because of process debt?

Why Rule-Based AI Beat Pure ML

My engineering background made me want to build a neural net. My product judgment said no.

Here's the thing about insurance: the domain has very clear rules encoded in policy documents. A Vitamin D test is covered under preventive wellness. An MRI for a pre-existing condition has a waiting period. A day-care procedure has a defined cost cap. These aren't fuzzy patterns — they're deterministic rules buried in PDFs.

A pure ML approach would have required thousands of labeled examples to learn what a policy document could have just told it directly. More importantly, when the model got something wrong — and it would — we'd have no explainability. An insurance company cannot tell a policyholder "the model decided."

The architecture we built was a hybrid rule-extraction + decision engine:

  1. Policy Parser: LLM-powered extraction of coverage rules from policy documents, structured into a queryable rule store
  2. Claim Classifier: ML model that tagged incoming claims by type, procedure, and policy category
  3. Rule Engine: Deterministic decision layer that matched classified claims against extracted rules
  4. Escalation Logic: Any claim the engine wasn't confident about (under a threshold) routed to a human adjudicator — but now with pre-populated context

The 70% Number

Seventy percent straight-through processing sounds like a nice round number. It wasn't chosen — it emerged. When we plotted claim complexity against volume, we found that roughly 70–75% of all claims were what I started calling "obvious claims" — ones where the rule was clear, the documentation was complete, and the right decision was not ambiguous.

These are the claims where a human adjudicator adds zero value. They're rubber-stamps, and having a human rubber-stamp them at scale was expensive, slow, and demoralizing for the adjudicators who actually wanted to use their judgment on hard cases.

The goal wasn't to replace adjusters. It was to give them only the cases that actually needed a human. When we shipped, adjudicator job satisfaction went up because they stopped spending 80% of their time on claims that didn't need them.

What Nearly Killed the Project

The hardest part wasn't the technology. It was getting trust alignment across three groups: the insurance companies (who worried about liability), the hospital networks (who worried about payment delays), and our own leadership (who worried about false positives).

The move that unlocked this was a shadow mode deployment. We ran the engine in parallel with human adjudicators for 6 weeks, comparing outputs without letting the engine's decision affect claims. The match rate was 94.2%. That number, more than any demo, was what got sign-off.

What I'd Tell Myself at the Start

  • Define "correct" before you build anything. In insurance, "correct" has a legal definition, an operational definition, and a customer perception definition. They're not the same.
  • Build for auditability from day one. Every automated decision needs to be explainable — not just for regulatory reasons, but for your own debugging.
  • Shadow mode is not optional. It's the only honest way to build trust with domain experts who've been burned by software promises before.
  • Your P(escalation) is a product decision, not a model parameter. How often the engine asks for human help is a policy choice with business consequences. Don't let the data science team set it alone.

The 10-second claim approval is a headline metric. The more meaningful number, to me, is the adjudicator who told me she now actually enjoys her job — because every claim that lands on her desk is genuinely hard, and she gets to use her expertise.

That's what "70% straight-through processing" actually means.
