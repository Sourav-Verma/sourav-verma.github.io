Building for Habits, Not Just Usage: Lessons from a 20K-User Health Platform

April 21, 2025 8 min read Growth, HealthTech, Engagement

Most health apps die in week two. The user downloads, completes onboarding, logs their first data point — and never returns. Not because the product is bad. Because health behavior change is genuinely hard, and most apps are designed for one-time action, not sustained habits.

When I was building the Chronic Disease Management (CDM) platform at Visit Health, I was acutely aware of this pattern. We had 0 users. We needed to get to meaningful scale — and to make it actually work for people managing real conditions like diabetes and hypertension. Vanity metrics wouldn't cut it. Neither would a feature list.

Here's what we built, what worked, and what didn't.

Why Most Health Apps Fail at Retention

The standard health app model is: give users a way to log things, show them graphs, send them reminders. This model is correct about what users need at a feature level. It's wrong about the psychology.

Health logging is intrinsically low-reward and high-effort. Logging your blood sugar is tedious. The graph it produces doesn't feel immediately meaningful unless you understand A1C trends. And reminder notifications — the classic engagement hack — are the first things users disable when they feel pestered.

📊 The Data

In early testing, we saw 68% of users who completed onboarding log at least once in week 1. By week 3, that number dropped to 12%. This is the classic "engagement cliff" in health apps.

The problem wasn't our reminders or our graph design. The problem was that we were optimizing for usage — opening the app, logging, viewing — rather than habit formation.

The Habit Loop Framework — with a HealthTech Twist

BJ Fogg's work on habit design comes down to three components: a cue (trigger), a routine (the behavior), and a reward (the reason the brain files this as "worth repeating"). The famous "Tiny Habits" insight is that motivation is unreliable — you have to design the behavior to be easier than the friction, not depend on willpower to overcome it.

For chronic disease management, we applied this with a few modifications:

1. Context-anchored cues, not time-based reminders

Instead of "10 AM reminder: log blood sugar," we shifted to event-triggered nudges: "You have a meal appointment in 30 minutes — would you like to log your pre-meal reading?" This feels like a service, not an alarm.

We integrated with calendar apps, medication reminders, and diagnostic lab orders. When we had a context signal — a doctor's appointment, a pharmacy refill, a reported symptom — we used it as a cue. When we didn't, we stayed quiet.

2. The minimum viable log

We obsessed over reducing friction on the core logging action. At one point, our blood glucose log was 4 taps. We got it to 1 — a single number input with smart defaults and auto-timestamp.

We also introduced "passive logging" for users who connected wearables, letting the platform infer context without requiring manual input. Engagement among connected users was 3.4× higher than among manual loggers.

3. Meaningful rewards, not gamification theater

Badges and streaks are engagement proxies. They don't mean the user's health is improving. We made a deliberate decision to tie rewards to health outcomes, not app activity.

"Your HbA1c has been in the healthy range for 3 weeks" is a more meaningful reward than a "7-day streak" badge — even though it requires waiting longer to deliver. We found that users who received outcome-based notifications had significantly better 90-day retention than those receiving only activity-based acknowledgment.

The Persona Architecture

One of the most valuable decisions we made early was to segment users by engagement archetype rather than medical condition. Our key personas:

  • The Anxious Optimizer: Logs everything, wants detailed analytics, highly responsive to data-driven insights
  • The Compliant Reluctant: Managing condition because they have to, not because they want to — highest dropout risk
  • The Community Seeker: Highly responsive to social proof, group challenges, peer comparison
  • The Delegator: Manages health through caregivers or family — product needs to support the caregiver as much as the patient

The same feature can hurt one persona while helping another. Streaks work brilliantly for Optimizers and actively damage Reluctants (who feel shame when they break a streak). We learned to surface different UX paths based on persona signals.

What 40% MoM Growth Actually Meant

The 40% month-over-month growth wasn't paid acquisition. It was almost entirely referral — users recommending the platform to family members who shared conditions, and corporate HR managers rolling it out after seeing early engagement data.

The most powerful growth lever turned out to be what we called the "health report card" — a monthly personalized summary a user could share with their doctor or family. It was designed as a clinical tool, but users were sharing it voluntarily on WhatsApp family groups. We hadn't designed for this. We just noticed it in analytics and got out of its way.

The best growth feature we shipped in the first year was one we didn't plan: a shareable health summary. We noticed users screenshotting it and built a real share flow. That single change drove a 22% increase in new user referrals.

The Lesson That Stuck

The engagement cliff isn't a UX problem. It's a motivation design problem. You cannot UX your way to retention if the core behavior doesn't have inherent meaning to the user.

For chronic disease management, that meaning exists — people want to manage their health. But it gets buried under friction, anxiety, and the tyranny of the streak. Our job was to unbury it: reduce the effort, connect the action to an outcome the user actually cares about, and give them a reason to come back that doesn't depend on habit energy they don't have at 8 AM on a Tuesday.

We got to 20K active users not because we built a better reminder system. We got there because we built something that felt like it was on the user's side.
